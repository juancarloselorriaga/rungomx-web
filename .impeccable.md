## Design Context

### Users

The public website should optimize first for athletes discovering events and deciding whether to register. They are comparing options, checking logistics, validating trust, and deciding whether an event feels legitimate, clear, and worth committing to. The public site also supports follow-up needs like schedules, results, and rankings, but the primary design lens is conversion through confidence.

### Brand Personality

Trustworthy, clear, grounded.

The interface should feel calm, premium, and dependable rather than hyped or flashy. It should reduce uncertainty, surface important facts quickly, and make registration feel safe, intentional, and well-organized.

### Aesthetic Direction

Premium editorial utility: calm, spacious, information-first.

Lean toward large-format typography, generous whitespace, restrained accents, and crisp section composition over dramatic marketing gestures or dense component chrome. Use the existing brand system in a disciplined way that supports scanning and trust. Avoid generic AI-startup aesthetics, especially decorative gradients, glow effects, interchangeable card grids, and over-framed surfaces that make the product feel templated instead of credible.

### Design Principles

1. Clarity before persuasion: decision-making information should appear before decorative storytelling.
2. Trust through proof: dates, location, pricing, organizer credibility, policies, and status should be easy to find and easy to verify.
3. Calm hierarchy: maintain a composed editorial structure with one clear primary action and muted secondary paths.
4. Whitespace as structure: create confidence through spacing, proportion, and pacing rather than extra borders, pills, and cards.
5. Section harmony: adjacent sections should feel composed within the same system, using consistent rhythm, alignments, and structural logic rather than unrelated layouts stacked together.
6. Progressive disclosure: reveal detail in layers so athletes can scan quickly, then dive deeper when needed.
7. Distinctive restraint: avoid generic AI landing-page patterns and use brand accents purposefully, not decoratively.
